# The Hidden Secrets of Japanese Numbers: Ancient Yamato Counting, Kotodama, and the Spiritual Power of Words

Have you ever wondered why Japanese people say "hatachi" (はたち) when referring to turning 20 years old? Why New Year's Eve is called "Ōmisoka" (大晦日), literally meaning "Big Thirtieth Day"? These words seem simple on the surface, but they hold secrets to one of Japan's oldest and most sacred traditions.

Today, I want to share a story about Japanese numbers—a tale that modern Japanese people are gradually forgetting. This isn't just about how to count. This is about how language carries the soul of an entire civilization.

---

## Why Does Japanese Have Two Counting Systems?

When I first encountered Japanese numbers, I was confused. Why were there so many different ways to say numbers?

The answer is simple: Japan has two worlds.

One is the Sino-Japanese system derived from Chinese—the one you probably learned when studying Japanese: ichi (一), ni (二), san (三). This system has been used in Japan for over 1,500 years. But long before that, the Japanese had their own way of counting.

**The ancient Yamato Kotoba (大和言葉) counting system** looks like this:

- 1. hitotsu (ひとつ)
- 2. futatsu (ふたつ)
- 3. mitsu (みつ)
- 4. yotsu (よつ)
- 5. itsutsu (いつつ)
- 6. mutsu (むつ)
- 7. nanatsu (ななつ)
- 8. yatsu (やつ)
- 9. kokonotsu (ここのつ)
- 10. tō (とお)

Look at these words. They have a rhythm, an ancient musicality. But there's something deeper beneath the surface.

---

## Every Number Has a Meaning: The Ancient Japanese Universe

This is where the story becomes fascinating. According to ancient Japanese tradition, each number represents more than just a quantity. It embodies the essence of something fundamental.

**Hi (ひ) = Sun (光)**
**Fu (ふ) = Wind (風)**
**Mi (み) = Water (水)**
**Yo (よ) = World, Life (世, 命)**
**I (い) = Reeds, Earth, Emergence (葦)**
**Mu (む) = Insects (蟲)**
**Na (な) = Fish (魚)**
**Ya (や) = Birds (鳥)**
**Ko (こ) = Animals (動物)**
**To (と) = Humanity (人間)**

Do you see it now? This isn't arbitrary assignment. The ancient Japanese used each number in their counting system to symbolize stages of creation.

In certain ancient Japanese traditions, this is called the **"Hifumi Norito"** (ひふみ祝詞)—an ancient incantation believed to contain the secrets of cosmic creation. According to traditional interpretation, the combination of these four words represents:

> Light appeared, and the sun was born. The wind began to blow. Water surrounded the world, and the seas were created. Thus our world was born. Life emerged.

This isn't a simple counting system. This is a creation story written in Japan's most ancient language.

---

## Why Is 20 Called "Hatachi"? And 30 Called "Misoji"? Unraveling the Age Mystery

This is my favorite part because it shows how ancient traditions still live in modern Japanese culture, even though most people don't realize it.

When Japanese people say you're 20 years old, they say "hatachi" (はたち). This comes from the ancient numbering system. Twenty is "hata" (はた)—a combination of two "tens" (tō). Now, when you know that "ten" originally represented "humanity," turning 20—the moment of reaching adulthood—becomes a ceremonially significant marker with profound spiritual meaning.

Similarly, 30 years old is called "misoji" (みそじ). The number 30 is "miso" (みそ). The Coming of Age ceremony (Seijin-shiki) is now held on the second Monday of January, but traditionally, 20 remains special—it's an actual milestone marked according to ancient Japanese numerals' true meaning.

These aren't arbitrary words. They're living linguistic fossils connecting us to a world we've almost forgotten.

---

## Yaoya (八百屋): Why Is a Grocery Store Called "800"?

Now let's jump to a word you've probably seen often but never questioned: **yaoya** (八百屋), which means "greengrocer" or "vegetable shop."

Why "800" (八百)?

Ancient Japanese used the number 800 to express "many" or "countless." This connects to a Shinto concept: **Yaoyorozu no Kami** (八百万の神) — literally "Eight Million Gods," but actually meaning "innumerable gods" or "gods everywhere."

In ancient Japanese belief, 8 is an auspicious number, valued in both Buddhism and Taoism. When combined with "hundred" (百), it creates the idea of infinite abundance. So a shop stocked with "many" products became known as "yaoya"—literally an "800 shop," but actually meaning "a place with all sorts of things."

This is a perfect example of how language carries cultural wisdom that transcends its literal meaning.

## Understanding Japanese Dialects: From Hokkaido to Okinawa

Japan's linguistic diversity is breathtaking. While many learners focus on standard Tokyo Japanese, the country boasts countless dialects that reflect centuries of regional development.

![Japanese Dialects Map](/images/japanese_dialects.png)

From the Hokkaido dialect (北海道方言) in the north to the unique Okinawan language (琉球方言) in the south, each region has preserved its own linguistic treasures. These dialects aren't just variations—they're repositories of local history, cultural values, and ancient wisdom.

---

## Kotodama: The Sacred Power of Japanese Language

Now we arrive at the heart of the story: **Kotodama** (言霊).

The concept of kotodama traces back to Japan's oldest religion—Shinto. It combines two words: "koto" (言 = language) and "tama" (霊 = spirit/soul).

**What does kotodama mean?** It's a belief that **words possess mystical power**. Not merely as tools for communication, but as entities with inherent energy and spiritual force.

In ancient Japan, it was believed that spoken words—especially if uttered correctly, at the right time, and with proper intention—could influence reality, heal the body, and even alter destiny. When you spoke someone's name aloud, that sound was believed to directly impact that person's soul.

This belief isn't outdated superstition. It persists in Japanese culture today:

- In martial arts, the "kiai" (気合) shout when attacking—believed to concentrate the warrior's resolve
- In New Year blessings using specific auspicious words
- In avoiding unlucky words (for example, Japanese people don't say "four" because it sounds like "death")

In the Oomoto religion and particularly through the teachings of Onisaburo Deguchi, kotodama was developed into a more sophisticated spiritual practice, suggesting that proper pronunciation and understanding of Japanese words held keys to spiritual enlightenment.

---

## The Deeper Significance: From Creation to Daily Life

What's remarkable about this ancient system is how it connected the abstract concept of numbers to the observable world. The Yamato counting system wasn't just a mathematical tool—it was a cosmological map embedded in language.

When an ancient Japanese person counted to ten, they weren't simply noting quantity. They were reciting a spiritual narrative:

- Light (ひ) brings forth the sun
- Wind (ふ) flows
- Water (み) creates life
- The world (よ) emerges
- Life (い) begins

This narrative structure reflects what many scholars call the **"Hifumi Norito" (Hifumi Prayer)**—an incantation of 47 hiragana characters that supposedly encodes fundamental universal principles. Each syllable carries symbolic weight, each sound resonates with cosmic meaning.

---

## Why Modern Japan Is Losing This—And What It Means

Here comes the melancholy part of the story.

As Japan modernized and westernized, particularly during the post-World War II Occupation, the ancient Yamato Kotoba counting system and its associated spiritual traditions gradually faded into obscurity.

Yet there's something encouraging: these words never completely disappeared. Japanese people still say "hatachi" to describe turning 20. They still say "Ōmisoka" on New Year's Eve. These words remain, waiting to be remembered.

In fact, looking at modern Japanese society, we can see kotodama principles still influence everyday choices—from lucky numbers in addresses to the careful selection of words for important occasions. The belief, whether conscious or unconscious, persists.

---

## Why Does This Still Matter?

In an age of social media, speed, and efficiency, taking time to learn an ancient counting system might seem irrelevant. But I'd like to offer a different perspective.

Language isn't merely a tool for communication. It's a container for culture. When a language dies—or when we forget its deeper layers—an entire worldview, a way of seeing the universe, disappears with it.

The ancient Japanese numerical system embedded a specific way of perceiving the world—as an interconnected cosmos where light, wind, water, life, and humanity all interweave in harmonious order. It's a worldview filled with spirituality and intention, not merely mechanical.

By learning these numbers, by understanding the meaning behind "Hifumi," by remembering the ancient traditions embedded in "hatachi," we're reconnecting with the wisdom of our ancestors—a wisdom that reminds us that every word matters, every sound carries weight, and language itself is sacred.

## Final Reflections

Next time you hear a Japanese person say "hatachi" or see "Ōmisoka" written down, pause for a moment. Remember what lies beneath—a linguistic thread connecting us to ancient Japan, to an era when words were believed to carry spiritual power, when numbers represented more than quantity, when language itself was understood as a force that shaped reality.

Perhaps, before we forget, this is something worth remembering.

And maybe—just maybe—in rediscovering these ancient words, we're also rediscovering something vital about what it means to be human: the power we all carry within our words, the responsibility we bear in speaking, and the sacredness that exists in language itself.

The ancient Japanese knew something we've largely forgotten. Perhaps it's time to listen.
